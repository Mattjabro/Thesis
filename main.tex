%--- Abstract -----------------------------------------------
\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

Soil harbors the most diverse microbial communities on Earth, yet whether predictable community types exist across biomes and whether taxonomic composition encodes habitat of origin remain open questions at global scale. This thesis addresses both questions by applying unsupervised clustering and supervised classification to transformed 16S ribosomal RNA (rRNA) amplicon profiles from two independent datasets: the global topsoil survey of Bahram et al.\cite{bahram2018} (193 samples) and the Earth Microbiome Project (EMP) soil subset of Thompson et al.\cite{thompson2017} (2,209 samples). Application of a sample clustering method based on a mixture of Gaussian Graphical Models (MixGGM) identified 19 clusters in the topsoil dataset and 10 in the EMP dataset; cluster assignments were significantly associated with biome type, geographic location, and physicochemical variables in both cases. Random forest classifiers trained on the same sample-taxa matrices achieved 61.8\% test-set accuracy under grouped biome labels for the topsoil data and 98.2\% for the EMP data. The performance gap likely reflects the underlying biology: major habitat boundaries (marine vs.\ terrestrial vs.\ freshwater) produce strong compositional contrasts, while within-terrestrial biome differences are subtler and driven by overlapping environmental gradients rather than discrete community turnover. The most predictive taxa, including \textit{Ellin516}, \textit{Candidatus Udaeobacter}, \textit{Anaeromyxobacter}, \textit{Bacillus}, and \textit{Paenibacillus}, have known ecological associations consistent with the biome categories they discriminated. Taken together, these results demonstrate that genus-level 16S rRNA profiles carry sufficient information to recover recurring community types and predict habitat of origin, with predictive power scaling with the breadth of environmental contrast in the dataset.

\chapter{Introduction}
%============================================================

Soil is among the most diverse microbial environments on Earth. A single gram can
harbor thousands of distinct bacterial taxa, and the collective activity of these communities
drives the biogeochemical cycles that underpin the function of terrestrial ecosystems globally
\cite{fierer2006}. Despite this importance, large-scale patterns in soil microbial
community structure remain difficult to characterize: whether predictable community types
exist across biomes and whether taxonomic composition encodes the environment of origin remain open
questions at global scale.

There are two DNA sequencing strategies for profiling these communities. Shotgun
metagenomics sequences DNA extracted from a soil sample, providing both functional and
taxonomic information but at increased cost and computational burden. 16S ribosomal RNA (rRNA) amplicon sequencing targets a phylogenetically conserved marker
gene present in all bacteria and archaea, allowing community-wide profiling, often at genus-level
resolution.

This thesis analyzes two independent global soil datasets. The first is the global topsoil
survey of Bahram et al.\cite{bahram2018}, comprising 193 samples after filtering, processed from raw
FASTQ files through the Divisive Amplicon Denoising Algorithm 2 (DADA2) pipeline\cite{callahan2016}. The second is the Earth Microbiome Project (EMP)
soil subset\cite{thompson2017}, comprising 2,209 samples drawn from a coordinated
multi-study archive. The datasets differ in scope, sequencing depth, and upstream processing
methodology; consistent findings in both indicate that results are robust to these differences.

What makes these analyses non-trivial is the compositional nature of amplicon count data:
read counts are constrained to sum to a fixed library size, making standard Euclidean
distances geometrically inappropriate and Pearson correlations spurious\cite{aitchison1986,gloor2017}. Soil microbiome data are
additionally high-dimensional and sparse, with thousands of lineages and many rare taxa
present in only a handful of samples. 

We ask two questions. First, can unsupervised clustering of suitable compositional data analysis transformation profiles reveal recurring community types without reference to sample
metadata? Second, does community composition allow a random forest classifier, trained on
biome labels, to predict habitat of origin, and which taxa carry the most predictive signal?

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Global Topsoil Dataset (Bahram et al.\ 2018)}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The global topsoil dataset originates from Bahram et al.\ (\textit{Nature}, 2018)\cite{bahram2018}, a
coordinated single-survey design in which composite soil cores were collected from 189 sites
spanning all major terrestrial biomes globally, producing 7,560 subsamples with matched
environmental metadata. The bacterial 16S rRNA gene V4 region was amplified using primers
515F/806RB and sequenced on Illumina platforms as paired-end reads; raw sequence data are
publicly archived under European Bioinformatics Institute (EBI) accession PRJEB19856 (ERP021922) as paired-end FASTQ files.
A total of 234 paired-end samples were downloaded for processing; 193 remained after
quality-based sample exclusions. Matched sample metadata, obtained from the SRA run table
(\texttt{sraRunTable.csv}), include biome type, country of collection, geographic coordinates
(latitude and longitude), elevation, soil pH, and mean annual temperature. For classification
analyses, the original biome categories were consolidated into seven broader groups (see
Section~\ref{sec:biome-grouping}). 

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{EMP Soil Subset (Thompson et al.\ 2017)}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The Earth Microbiome Project (EMP) dataset originates from Thompson et al.\
(\textit{Nature}, 2017)\cite{thompson2017}, which aggregated and harmonized 16S rRNA amplicon data from
97 independent studies comprising 27,751 samples spanning diverse environment types
worldwide. EMP Release 1 data were obtained from Zenodo as three files: the Deblur count
table
\\
(\texttt{emp\_deblur\_150bp\_release1.biom}), a representative FASTA file of all unique
sequences (\texttt{emp\_deblur\_150bp\_min25.fasta}), and the comprehensive metadata table
(\texttt{emp\_metadata\_release1\_20170912.tsv}). Soil samples were extracted by filtering on
the \texttt{env\_material} metadata field using the regular expression
\\
\texttt{soil|sediment|rhizosphere}, yielding 2,209 samples. Available metadata include Earth
Microbiome Project Ontology (EMPO) environment classification at three hierarchical levels,
Environmental Ontology (ENVO) biome descriptors at four levels, and continuous
physicochemical measurements including pH, temperature, salinity, phosphate, nitrate,
ammonium, elevation, and geographic coordinates. For classification analyses, biome
annotations were consolidated into a 10-group scheme (see Section~\ref{sec:biome-grouping}).

\section{Computational Environment}
%------------------------------------------------------------

All analyses were performed on the Hopper high performance computer (HPC) cluster at
Claremont McKenna College. Compute nodes provide up to
128 CPUs and either 750 GB RAM or 3 TB RAM (high-memory nodes). Each analytical step is
wrapped in a dedicated SLURM\cite{yoo2003} batch script and output files from each step are written to
structured subdirectories to ensure reproducibility and auditability of the full pipeline.

R 4.2.0 (deployed via a dedicated \texttt{r-dada2} conda environment) was the primary
language throughout, with the following packages providing core functionality: DADA2\cite{callahan2016} for amplicon denoising; Biostrings and phyloseq\cite{mcmurdie2013} for sequence and count-table handling; vegan\cite{dixon2003} for alpha diversity metrics; randomForest\cite{breiman2001} and caret for classifier training and train/test partitioning; Rtsne\cite{maaten2008} for non-linear dimensionality reduction;
pheatmap and ggplot2 for visualization; and tidyverse for data manipulation throughout.
Cluster metadata visualization was implemented in Python using pandas, seaborn, and
matplotlib. All analysis scripts, SLURM batch files, and pipeline documentation are available at \url{https://github.com/Mattjabro/Thesis}.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Sequence Processing — Global Topsoil (DADA2 Pipeline)}
\label{sec:dada2}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Raw paired-end FASTQ files were processed through a DADA2 amplicon sequence variant
(ASV) pipeline in R on an HPC cluster. The full pipeline structure is shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:1}; per-step read retention statistics are provided in Supplementary Table~1.
The SILVA v138.2 genus-level reference\cite{quast2013} was pre-filtered
to remove Eukaryota, Chloroplast, and Mitochondria entries prior to taxonomy assignment.
Reads were quality-filtered with \texttt{filterAndTrim()} (maximum errors set to 2, minimum
quality cutoff Q\,=\,2). Sequencing error models were estimated independently for forward and
reverse reads with \texttt{learnErrors()}, and denoising was performed with \texttt{dada()}.
Denoised reads were merged with \texttt{mergePairs()}, and samples with inadequate overlap
were excluded. An ASV count matrix was constructed with \texttt{makeSequenceTable()} and
chimeric sequences removed with \texttt{removeBimeraDenovo()}. Non-bacterial ASVs
(chloroplasts, mitochondria, eukaryotic 18S) were removed, and per-sample read counts were
tracked across all stages as a quality-control (QC) check.

Taxonomy was assigned against the pre-filtered SILVA v138.2 reference to genus level
with \texttt{assignTaxonomy()}. Prevalence filtering and CLR transformation are described
in Sections~\ref{sec:prevalence} and~\ref{sec:clr} respectively.

\section{Sequence Processing — EMP Soil Subset}
\label{sec:emp-processing}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The EMP soil subset data used was an ASV table denoised with Deblur\cite{amir2017} at a
fixed 150 bp read length, accompanied by a representative FASTA file of all unique sequences
and a comprehensive metadata table. 

Soil samples were extracted by filtering the metadata on \texttt{env\_material} using the
regular expression \texttt{soil|sediment|rhizosphere}. The Biological Observation Matrix (BIOM) count table was converted to
TSV with \texttt{biom convert} and parsed in R to a dense ASV~$\times$~sample matrix.
Because the EMP release uses internal sequence identifiers rather than taxonomy strings,
taxonomy was assigned \textit{de novo} with the SILVA v138.1 genus-level reference. ASV-level
counts were then collapsed to genus level by summing counts sharing a resolved genus
assignment; entries with unresolved (NA) genus were discarded. Samples present in the
metadata but absent from the Deblur count matrix were inserted as all-zero rows. The full processing pipeline is shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:2}; key statistics are provided in Supplementary Table~2.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Quality Control}
\label{sec:qc}
%------------------------------------------------------------

For the global topsoil dataset, per-sample ASV richness was computed directly from the
post-chimera DADA2 object. Row sums and per-sample genus richness of the genus-collapsed
matrix were also computed and exported. Read counts through all DADA2 stages were tracked.

For the EMP dataset, a read count vs.\ genus count comparison verified that
genus-collapsed counts summed consistently with raw read totals. A cross-reference check
confirmed that all ASV identifiers in the representative FASTA, taxonomy table, and count
matrix were mutually consistent.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Prevalence Filtering and Threshold Analysis}
\label{sec:prevalence}
%------------------------------------------------------------

A prevalence filter was applied to both datasets prior to CLR transformation to reduce
dimensionality while preserving compositional signal. A sweep over minimum-prevalence values
was performed for each dataset, computing at each cutoff the fraction of lineages retained and
the fraction of total abundance signal retained. A threshold of 20 samples was selected for the
global topsoil dataset, and 50 for the EMP dataset.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{CLR Matrix Construction}
\label{sec:clr}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The centered log-ratio (CLR) transformation\cite{aitchison1986,gloor2017} maps
compositional data into an unrestricted space, providing the geometrically appropriate metric
for compositional data analysis. The transformation pipeline is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:3}.

\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CLR}(X_{ij}) = \log(X_{ij}+1) - \frac{1}{D}\sum_{k=1}^{D}\log(X_{kj}+1)
  \label{eq:clr}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $X_{ij}$ is the raw read count of lineage $i$ in sample $j$, and $D$ is the
total number of lineages (rows).

For the global topsoil dataset, the post-chimera bacteria-only ASV matrix was collapsed
to genus level, and samples with fewer than 5,000 total counts were removed. For the EMP
dataset, the genus-collapsed matrix from Section~\ref{sec:emp-processing} served as input
directly; samples with fewer than 5,000 total counts were likewise removed. In both cases,
the prevalence filter from Section~\ref{sec:prevalence} was applied, and a pseudocount of 1
was added to all entries to handle structural zeros.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering

    Centered log-ratio (CLR) transformation pipeline\cite{aitchison1986,gloor2017}, applied identically to both datasets. Input: sparse

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Sample Clustering}
\label{sec:gmm}
%------------------------------------------------------------

A mixture of Gaussian Graphical Models (MixGGM) framework\cite{yooseph2022} was applied to both CLR-transformed feature matrices to identify
recurring community types. The model was initialized with K\,=\,20 components as an upper
bound; components with negligible mixing coefficients become unpopulated after convergence,
so the effective number of clusters is determined by which components retain sample
assignments. For the global topsoil dataset, the model was fit using 25 random initializations
and 150 expectation-maximization (EM) iterations, retaining the solution with the highest
evidence lower bound (ELBO) across all starts. For the EMP dataset, computational constraints
required reducing the fitting procedure to 5 random initializations and 10 EM iterations. Each
sample receives a posterior probability of membership in each of the K components; hard cluster
labels were assigned by taking the argmax posterior membership probability and exported for
all downstream analyses.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Cluster Characterization by Metadata}
\label{sec:cluster-char}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Cluster assignments were joined to sample metadata on sample identifiers to produce an
annotated table linking each sample's cluster label to all available environmental variables
(Supplementary Table~3). For the global topsoil dataset this included biome type, country,
geographic coordinates, elevation, soil pH, and mean annual temperature. For the EMP dataset
it included EMPO classification at three hierarchical levels, ENVO biome descriptors at four
levels, and continuous physicochemical measurements. To characterize the taxonomic signature
of each cluster, the mean CLR value for every lineage was computed across all samples assigned
to that cluster, producing a lineages\,$\times$\,clusters matrix of average CLR abundances.
Stacked bar plots of biome composition per cluster, strip plots of continuous variables by
cluster, and a lineages\,$\times$\,clusters heatmap of mean CLR values were generated. The
full pipeline for cluster characterization, alpha diversity, and enhanced CLR statistics is
shown in Figure~\ref{fig:4}.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Alpha Diversity Analysis}
\label{sec:alpha}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Three alpha diversity metrics were computed from the bacteria-only post-chimera ASV
matrix (global topsoil) and from the genus-collapsed count matrix (EMP): observed richness,
Chao1 bias-corrected richness\cite{chao1984}, and Shannon entropy\cite{shannon1948}. These were computed using the vegan package\cite{dixon2003}. Cluster assignments were joined to the resulting per-sample table and
summary statistics (mean, SD, median) computed per cluster.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Ordination}
\label{sec:ordination}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Three ordination methods were applied to the CLR matrices. Principal Component
Analysis (PCA)\cite{pearson1901} was performed using \texttt{prcomp()} in
R, retaining 20 components. Principal Coordinate Analysis (PCoA)\cite{gower1966} was computed using \texttt{cmdscale()} and used as a consistency check; PCoA on
CLR-transformed data is mathematically equivalent to CLR-PCA. t-distributed Stochastic
Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE)\cite{maaten2008} was applied using Rtsne to capture non-linear structure not visible in linear
projections. Sample scores from all three methods were exported alongside cluster labels and
biome codes for visualization.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Biome Grouping}
\label{sec:biome-grouping}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Specific biome annotations were consolidated into broader ecological groups prior to
classification to reduce class imbalance (Supplementary Table~5). For the global topsoil
dataset, 11 original biome categories were collapsed into 7 groups organized by thermal
and vegetation structure. For the EMP dataset, they were collapsed to 10 groups.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Random Forest Biome Classification}
\label{sec:rf}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Random forest classifiers\cite{breiman2001} were trained on
CLR-transformed composition profiles to predict biome of origin (Supplementary Table~5).
Classes with fewer than 10 samples were excluded, and the dataset was split 70/30 into
stratified train and test sets using caret. Separate models were trained for original and grouped
biome labels, yielding four models in total. Test-set performance was assessed by accuracy,
Cohen's kappa, and macro-averaged precision, recall, and F1. Feature importance was extracted
as MeanDecreaseAccuracy (MDA) and MeanDecreaseGini for all lineages; the top 50 lineages
by permutation importance were noted.

\section{Sequence Processing and Quality Control — Global Topsoil}
\label{res:seqproc}
%------------------------------------------------------------

A total of 234 paired-end FASTQ file pairs were downloaded from EBI accession
PRJEB19856 and processed through the DADA2 denoising pipeline. The 234 samples together
contributed 33,170,669 raw input reads. Quality filtering and primer trimming with
\texttt{filterAndTrim()} were followed by paired-end merging with \texttt{mergePairs()}.
After ASV table construction and chimeric sequence removal with
\texttt{removeBimeraDenovo()}, a mean 33.4\% of input reads were retained as non-chimeric
across all samples. Non-bacterial ASVs (chloroplasts, mitochondria, eukaryotic 18S) were
removed, and 41 samples with fewer than 5,000 non-chimeric reads were excluded. The final analysis set comprised 193 samples spanning 38 countries
and latitudes from $-54.8^{\circ}$ to $78.2^{\circ}$. Per-step read counts for all samples are
provided in Supplementary Table~1. The per-sample read retention trajectory across all DADA2
stages is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:5}.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Prevalence Filtering and Feature Retention}
\label{res:prevalence}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Prior to CLR transformation, genus-level lineages present in fewer than a minimum
number of samples were removed to reduce noise and dimensionality. Threshold sweep curves
plotting the fraction of lineages retained and the fraction of total read counts retained as a
function of minimum prevalence are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:6}.

For the global topsoil dataset, a threshold of 20 samples removed the majority of
low-prevalence lineages while preserving approximately 97.5\% of total read counts. The
threshold of 20 samples was approximately 10\% of the 193-sample dataset, and retained
165 genus-level lineages.

samples) was applied, retaining 1,533 lineages (942 genus-resolved and 591 partial-taxonomy
lineages at phylum, class, order, or family level) and approximately 98.7\% of the read counts.

The final CLR-transformed feature matrices used in all downstream analyses were
193 samples\,$\times$\,165 lineages (global topsoil) and 2,209 samples\,$\times$\,1,533
lineages (EMP soil subset).

\section{Biome Classification}
\label{res:rf}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Random Forest classifiers were trained to predict biome of origin from CLR-transformed
microbial composition. Four classifiers were trained in total, one for each combination of
dataset (global topsoil, EMP soil subset) and label scheme (original, grouped biomes).
Test-set performance metrics and full feature importance rankings are provided in
Supplementary Table~5.

For the global topsoil dataset, eight original biome classes were retained for classifier
training; consolidation into grouped labels reduced this to six classes after exclusion of Arctic
Tundra ($n\,=\,1$). Under original biome labels, the Random Forest achieved 43.75\%
test-set accuracy (Cohen's $\kappa\,=\,0.327$). Grouping biomes into six broader categories
improved accuracy to 61.82\% ($\kappa\,=\,0.441$), with Grassland/Shrubland and
Mediterranean categories excluded before splitting due to having fewer than 10 samples, and
therefore not contributing to the grouped accuracy estimate. Within the original label model, the moist tropical forest class
showed the highest recall of the larger classes (63.6\%), while the southern temperate forest
class showed the lowest recall (14.3\%), with misclassification distributed across multiple
other temperate categories. Under grouped labels, the Tropical Forest group showed the best
recall of the four evaluable classes (75.0\%), followed by Temperate Forest (55.9\%). The most
predictive lineages by permutation importance (Mean Decrease Accuracy) under original biome labels were
\textit{Ellin516} (MDA\,=\,7.97), \textit{Tundrisphaera} (MDA\,=\,7.80), and
\textit{Pedomicrobium} (MDA\,=\,7.73). Under grouped labels, \textit{Ellin516}
(MDA\,=\,7.47), \textit{Anaeromyxobacter} (MDA\,=\,7.31), and \textit{Bacillus}
(MDA\,=\,6.58) ranked highest. Top lineage importances for both topsoil models are shown
in Figure~\ref{fig:12}.

For the EMP soil subset, the same architecture was applied to 11 original and 9 grouped
biome classes. Test-set accuracy was 97.11\% under original biome labels ($\kappa\,=\,0.961$) and 98.17\%
under grouped labels ($\kappa\,=\,0.975$). The confusion matrix for original biome labels
showed near-perfect diagonal results for most classes: marine (92 of 92), montane grassland
(68 of 68), urban (252 of 252), small river (13 of 13), and tropical moist broadleaf forest
(22 of 22) were all classified with perfect test-set recall. The cropland class showed the most
misclassification (12 of 183 test-set cropland samples were misclassified, primarily predicted as rangeland), and tundra samples
showed the second-highest error rate (3 of 9 test samples predicted as freshwater). The top
predictive lineages under original biome labels were \textit{Sphingomonas} (MDA\,=\,10.76),
\textit{Paenibacillus} (MDA\,=\,10.67), and \textit{Candidatus} Udaeobacter
(MDA\,=\,9.39). Under grouped labels, \textit{Paenibacillus} (MDA\,=\,10.19),
ADurb.Bin063-1 (MDA\,=\,9.54), and \textit{Planococcaceae} (MDA\,=\,9.46) ranked
highest. Top lineage importances for both EMP models are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:12}.

\chapter{Discussion}
%============================================================

This thesis set out to address two questions: whether unsupervised clustering of
CLR-transformed 16S rRNA profiles can recover recurring microbial community types without
reference to environmental metadata, and whether microbial composition carries enough
predictive signal for a supervised classifier to identify a sample's biome of origin. Both
questions received affirmative answers, with important caveats that depend on the scale and
environmental diversity of the dataset. Taken together, results from the global topsoil survey and the EMP soil subset suggest that soil microbial communities are shaped by environmental conditions, but not fully determined by them: community composition
tracks habitat, but considerable overlap between biomes means that composition alone is an
imperfect fingerprint.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Unsupervised Clustering Recovers Environmentally Coherent Groupings}
\label{disc:clustering}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The MixGGM clustering \cite{yooseph2022} identified 19 non-empty
components in the 193-sample global topsoil dataset and 10 in the 2,209-sample EMP soil
subset. These clusters were not random partitions. In both datasets, cluster assignments were
significantly associated with biome labels, geographic location, and, where available,
physicochemical measurements such as pH and salinity (Supplementary Tables~3--4). The fact
that an algorithm operating purely on CLR-transformed abundance profiles, with no access
to metadata, recovered groupings that correspond to independently annotated habitat
categories constitutes evidence that compositional structure in soil microbial communities is
shaped by environment in a manner detectable even at genus-level resolution.

The strength of this correspondence differed between the two datasets. In the EMP soil subset,
where samples spanned a much wider environmental gradient (from marine sediment to
agricultural soil to freshwater systems), cluster-metadata associations were considerably
stronger. The Cram\'{e}r's $V$ of 0.822 for EMPO level-2 classification against cluster
assignment indicates that the unsupervised groupings tracked habitat type closely. Marine
clusters (C20, C11) separated cleanly from terrestrial clusters, and the freshwater cluster
(C17) was compositionally distinct from both. This makes ecological sense: the transition
from saline to non-saline environments selects for fundamentally different microbial
assemblages, and that difference overwhelms the more similar within-terrestrial habitats.
Indeed, salinity has been identified as a primary driver of global microbial biogeography
\cite{lozupone2007}, and the EMP clustering reflects this.

In the global topsoil dataset, where all samples came from terrestrial soils, the
cluster-biome association was weaker but still highly significant (Cram\'{e}r's
$V = 0.485$, $p = 6.1 \times 10^{-28}$). No single cluster was exclusively composed of
one biome. Cluster~17 ($n = 39$) was dominated by tropical forest samples, and Cluster~16
($n = 18$) was enriched for boreal and coniferous environments, but every cluster contained
at least some mixture of biome types. This partial overlap is consistent with what is known
about soil microbial communities at the global scale: biome labels capture vegetation
structure and climate, but microbial communities respond to a combination of factors,
like soil pH, that do not always align neatly with biome boundaries
\cite{fierer2006,lauber2009}. Two soils
from different biomes but with similar pH values may harbor more similar microbial
communities than two soils from the same biome at different pH values. The clustering
captures this: it finds compositional groupings that track biome imperfectly precisely
because composition is shaped by a combination of environmental factors, not by biome
identity alone.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Alpha Diversity Varies Across Community Types}
\label{disc:alpha}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The alpha diversity analysis showed that the clusters identified by MixGGM differed in
their within-sample taxonomic richness and evenness. In the global topsoil dataset, mean
observed genus richness ranged from 244 (Cluster~9) to 709 (Cluster~20), and Shannon
entropy varied from 5.18 to 6.23 across these same clusters. The low diversity of
Cluster~9, which contained four samples, may reflect sampling from environments with
strong abiotic filters, such as extreme pH or low organic matter, that constrain the pool
of taxa able to colonize and persist. In contrast, Cluster~20, with the highest richness,
drew from samples in temperate and southern temperate forests where moderate pH and higher
organic carbon availability may support a broader range of taxa.

The EMP dataset showed an even wider spread, with mean observed richness ranging from 289
(Cluster~10) to 5,402 (Cluster~5). Cluster~10 was predominantly marine sediment, an
environment where the combination of high salinity and anaerobic conditions is known to
constrain bacterial diversity relative to terrestrial soils
\cite{thompson2017}. Cluster~5, with the highest richness,
corresponded to terrestrial samples from varied soil environments. This discrepancy is
consistent with the ecological observation that terrestrial soils, and particularly
those with moderate pH, tend to harbor the highest bacterial diversity of any environment
on Earth \cite{fierer2006}. The fact that Chao1 richness estimates
tracked observed richness closely across all clusters in both datasets suggests that
sampling depth was generally adequate for the genus-level resolution used here; the unseen
fraction was small relative to the detected community.

These differences are meaningful because diversity provides an independent ecological metric not used in the clustering procedure. They suggest that the unsupervised clustering is not merely driven by overall library size or sequencing noise. Instead, the clusters reflect biologically meaningful differences in community structure that are also captured by diversity metrics computed from the raw count data prior to CLR transformation.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Ordination Confirms Separation but Reveals Continuous Gradients}
\label{disc:ordination}
%------------------------------------------------------------

PCA of the CLR-transformed matrices provided a complementary view of community structure.
In the global topsoil dataset, the first principal component explained 20.0\% of variance
and the first ten components together explained 50.7\%. This moderate concentration of
variance across many components indicates that soil microbial composition varies along
multiple independent axes rather than being dominated by a single environmental gradient.
The PCA biplot showed partial separation along PC1, with Arctic Tundra samples occupying the negative extreme and Tropical Forest samples tending toward the positive end, but with substantial overlap among the remaining biomes in the center of the plot. This pattern is consistent with a continuous environmental gradient, most likely driven by temperature and its correlates, rather than discrete community types with sharp boundaries.

In the EMP soil subset, the variance structure was markedly different. PC1 alone accounted
for 41.4\% of total variance, nearly twice the proportion observed in the topsoil dataset.
This strong first axis reflects the dominant marine-versus-terrestrial split: samples from
saline environments clustered tightly on one end of PC1, with terrestrial samples
distributed along the remainder. The concentration of variance in PC1 in the EMP dataset,
compared to the more diffuse distribution in the topsoil dataset, is a direct consequence
of the wider environmental scope. When both marine and terrestrial samples are present,
the salinity variability overwhelms the finer signals that differentiate one terrestrial biome
from another.

The $t$-SNE projections reinforced these observations in a nonlinear space. In the EMP dataset,
$t$-SNE produced clearly separated islands corresponding to MixGGM clusters, particularly
for marine and freshwater samples. In the topsoil dataset, the separation was less distinct,
with clusters overlapping in the two-dimensional projection. This is not a failure of the
method: it accurately reflects the underlying data structure, where terrestrial soil communities change gradually along environmental gradients rather than separating into discrete types.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Random Forest Classification: Composition Encodes Habitat}
\label{disc:rf}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The Random Forest classifiers provided the supervised complement to the unsupervised
clustering, testing whether community composition carries enough information to predict
biome of origin when labels are available for training. The results differed dramatically
between datasets. On the EMP soil subset, classification accuracy was 97.1\% under original
biome labels and 98.2\% under grouped labels, with near-perfect recall for marine, urban,
and tropical forest classes. On the global topsoil dataset, accuracy was 43.8\% under
original labels and 61.8\% under grouped labels.

This contrast is not paradoxical. The EMP dataset included major environment types
(marine, freshwater, urban, agricultural, tundra) that differ from one another in
physicochemical conditions far more than the biomes within the terrestrial topsoil dataset
differ from each other. Classifying a marine sediment sample as marine on the basis of its
microbial community is, in a sense, straightforward: the microbial assemblage of a saline,
anoxic marine sediment shares very few genera with an arable field. The topsoil dataset,
by contrast, asks the classifier to distinguish moist tropical forest from dry tropical
forest, or southern temperate forest from temperate coniferous forest; biomes that share
substantial portions of their bacterial communities and whose compositional differences
are subtle.

The grouped-label models improved accuracy in both datasets, though the improvement was
more meaningful for the topsoil data (from 43.8\% to 61.8\%). Collapsing fine-grained
biome categories into broader groups such as Tropical Forest, Temperate Forest, and Boreal
Forest reduced the number of classes the model needed to discriminate and increased the
sample size per class, both of which favor better performance. Even so, the 61.8\% accuracy
in the topsoil data means the classifier made errors on roughly four of every ten test
samples. This is itself informative: it quantifies the degree to which biome identity is
encoded in genus-level microbial composition at global scale, and suggests that while the
signal is present, it is not dominant. Local factors such as pH, moisture, soil texture, 
and land use history introduce enough variation within biomes to blur the compositional
boundaries between them.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Predictive Taxa and Their Ecological Context}
\label{disc:taxa}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The taxa identified as most important by Random Forest permutation importance provide
biologically interpretable connections between microbial composition and habitat. Across the four
models (two datasets $\times$ two label schemes), several genera
appeared repeatedly, and their known ecology is consistent with the biome associations
detected.

\textit{Ellin516} (Verrucomicrobiota), the top-ranked lineage in both topsoil models, is a
member of a phylum that is abundant in soils globally but remains poorly represented in
culture collections\cite{bergmann2011}. Verrucomicrobiota are
involved in the degradation of complex plant-derived polysaccharides through the production
of cellulases and xylanases, and their relative abundance is known to vary with vegetation
type and soil organic matter quality\cite{navarrete2015}. The fact that
\textit{Ellin516} carried the strongest signal for biome discrimination in the topsoil
dataset suggests that differences in litter chemistry between biomes are reflected in the
abundance of polysaccharide-degrading Verrucomicrobiota. Tropical forests, for example,
produce more recalcitrant leaf litter than temperate deciduous forests.

\textit{Candidatus Udaeobacter} (Verrucomicrobiota), which ranked among the top predictive
lineages in the EMP models, is one of the most abundant soil bacteria on Earth despite
having an unusually small, streamlined genome (${\sim}$2.8~Mbp) with multiple amino acid
and vitamin auxotrophies\cite{brewer2017}. Its abundance is highest
in grassland soils and it is thought to rely on nutrients released by neighboring organisms
rather than synthesizing them \textit{de novo}\cite{willms2020}. The
ecological strategy of \textit{Ca.}\ Udaeobacter, an oligotrophic scavenger with a
minimized genome, makes it a sensitive indicator of soil conditions, and its differential
abundance across the EMP biome categories is consistent with its known preference for
well-drained, neutral pH terrestrial soils.

\textit{Anaeromyxobacter} (Myxococcota), the second-ranked genus in the grouped topsoil
model, is a facultative anaerobe capable of iron and nitrate reduction that thrives in
waterlogged soils\cite{treude2003,thomas2008}.
Its high importance for biome classification in the topsoil dataset likely reflects its
differential abundance between the poorly drained tropical forest soils (where periods
of low oxygen are common) and the well-drained temperate and boreal soils where its niche
is less available.

\textit{Bacillus} (Bacillota), ranked third in the grouped topsoil model, is among the
most metabolically versatile genera in soil, capable of forming endospores that allow
persistence under drought and temperature extremes\cite{nicholson2002}.
Its importance in the classifier may reflect the variable selective pressures across biomes; \textit{Bacillus}-dominated communities may be favored in drier grassland and savanna
soils where sporulation provides a survival advantage.

In the EMP models, \textit{Paenibacillus} (Firmicutes) and \textit{Sphingomonas}
(Pseudomonadota) ranked first and fifth, respectively, under grouped labels.
\textit{Paenibacillus} is commonly associated with agricultural and rhizosphere soils,
where it contributes to nitrogen fixation and phosphate solubilization
\cite{grady2016}. \textit{Sphingomonas} is a metabolically versatile
genus found across diverse soil environments but often enriched in anthropogenically
disturbed soils\cite{asaf2020}. Their high predictive value in the EMP models reflects the inclusion of agricultural and urban soils, where human disturbance favors different microbial functional groups than those found in undisturbed natural environments.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Convergence of Supervised and Unsupervised Approaches}
\label{disc:convergence}
%------------------------------------------------------------

A central question motivating this thesis was whether unsupervised and supervised analyses
of the same compositional data would yield consistent conclusions. They do; the MixGGM
clusters, derived without any metadata, recovered groupings that correspond closely to
biome labels (the same labels that the Random Forest used as training targets). The taxa
that the Random Forest identified as most predictive of biome identity are also the taxa
whose CLR values varied most across MixGGM clusters. This convergence across
methodologically independent approaches strengthens the conclusion that global soil
microbial communities are structured by environment in a manner that is encoded in
genus-level 16S rRNA profiles.

The fact that both approaches succeeded more clearly on the EMP dataset than on the global
topsoil dataset is itself meaningful. It demonstrates that the strength of compositional
structuring scales with the breadth of environmental contrast. When the sample set spans a
wide gradient, from ocean sediment to tundra to cropland, community types are well
defined and classification is nearly perfect. When the sample set covers terrestrial soils
only, the compositional signal is weaker, clusters are fuzzier, and classification accuracy
is moderate. This is not a limitation of the analytical methods but rather a property of the
biological system: soil microbial communities across terrestrial biomes share many widespread genera, and differences between biomes arise mainly from shifts in relative abundance rather than wholesale turnover of taxa.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Limitations}
\label{disc:limitations}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Several limitations of this analysis should be noted. First, 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing
provides taxonomic information only and cannot directly resolve community function. Two
communities with similar genus-level profiles may differ in their functional gene content,
and conversely, functionally similar communities may differ taxonomically. Shotgun
metagenomics would provide complementary functional resolution but at the cost of reduced
sample throughput and increased computational burden.

Second, the two datasets were processed through different upstream pipelines: DADA2 for
the global topsoil data and Deblur for the EMP soil subset. This introduces a potential
source of pipeline-specific bias in the inferred amplicon sequence variants (ASVs). This was
mitigated by collapsing both datasets to genus level and applying the same CLR
transformation and downstream analyses, but subtle differences in how the two denoising
algorithms handle sequencing error and chimeras cannot be entirely excluded as a
confounding factor.

Third, the MixGGM model was initialized with $K = 20$ components for both datasets, and
the number of populated clusters after convergence was determined by the data. For the EMP
dataset, computational constraints required reducing the number of random initializations
and EM iterations relative to the topsoil dataset. While the retained solution was selected
by the highest evidence lower bound (ELBO), it is possible that additional random starts
would yield a solution with higher likelihood or different cluster structure, particularly
given the high dimensionality of the EMP feature matrix (1,533 lineages).

Fourth, the global topsoil dataset, despite its global geographic coverage, is relatively
small (193 samples after filtering). Small per-biome sample sizes, like a single Arctic
tundra sample, for example, limit the statistical power to characterize rare biome types.

Finally, the prevalence filtering thresholds (20 samples for the topsoil data, 50 for the
EMP data) necessarily excluded rare taxa that may be ecologically important in specific
habitats. The CLR transformation with a pseudocount of $+1$ handles structural zeros, but
the information lost by removing low-prevalence lineages cannot be recovered downstream.
This is a common tradeoff in compositional data analysis: retaining very rare taxa increases
dimensionality and noise, while removing them risks discarding biologically relevant signal.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Future Direction}
\label{disc:outlook}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The analytical framework developed here---CLR transformation, MixGGM clustering, and
Random Forest classification applied to the same feature matrix---could be extended in
several directions. Incorporating functional gene data from shotgun metagenomics would
allow the community types identified here to be linked to specific metabolic capabilities,
testing whether the compositional clusters correspond to functionally distinct microbiome
states. Applying the MixGGM framework to time-series soil sampling would reveal whether
community types are stable over seasonal or interannual cycles or whether samples shift
between cluster assignments in response to changing conditions. Additionally, the Random
Forest feature importances identified here could inform targeted cultivation or
amplicon-based monitoring efforts: genera like \textit{Ellin516} and
\textit{Candidatus Udaeobacter}, which carry strong predictive signal but remain poorly
characterized, are high-priority targets for further genomic and physiological study.

%============================================================
\chapter{Conclusion}
%============================================================

This thesis applied a consistent analytical framework: centered log-ratio transformation, mixture of Gaussian Graphical Models clustering, and random forest classification to two independent global soil microbiome datasets derived from 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing. The global topsoil dataset\cite{bahram2018} provided 193 samples from a single coordinated survey of terrestrial biomes, while the Earth Microbiome Project soil subset\cite{thompson2017} provided 2,209 samples spanning terrestrial, marine, and freshwater environments across 97 independent studies.

Unsupervised clustering recovered environmentally coherent community groupings in both datasets without access to sample metadata. In the global topsoil dataset, 19 populated clusters showed significant associations with biome type, country, and latitude, though no cluster was exclusively composed of a single biome. In the EMP soil subset, 10 populated clusters separated sharply by habitat type, with marine, freshwater, and terrestrial samples occupying distinct clusters. The strength of cluster-metadata correspondence scaled with the breadth of environmental contrast represented in the dataset: wider environmental gradients produced cleaner separations.

Random forest classifiers trained on the same CLR-transformed profiles achieved 61.8\% accuracy on the global topsoil dataset under grouped biome labels and 98.2\% on the EMP soil subset. The contrast reflects the underlying biology: distinguishing marine sediment from agricultural soil is a fundamentally easier classification problem than separating moist tropical forest from dry tropical forest on the basis of genus-level composition alone. The taxa carrying the strongest predictive signal: \textit{Ellin516} and \textit{Candidatus Udaeobacter} (Verrucomicrobiota), \textit{Anaeromyxobacter} (Myxococcota), \textit{Bacillus} (Bacillota), and \textit{Paenibacillus} (Firmicutes), have known ecological associations with the biome categories they discriminated, providing biological grounding for the statistical results.

The convergence of supervised and unsupervised approaches on consistent conclusions strengthens the central finding: soil microbial communities are structured by environment in a manner encoded in genus-level 16S rRNA profiles. That structure is strongest across major habitat boundaries and weaker, though still detectable, within terrestrial biomes where communities share many of the same genera and differ primarily in relative abundance.
